SAFER SEX

In this category, we focus on how to have safer sex and gain people’s awareness about STD or even birth control, two important factors that people should be aware of.

STD Symptoms for Girls
Chlamydia Most women do not experience any symptoms. some experience vaginal discharge, abdominal pain. It can cause severe pelvic infection, which can lead to infertility and tubal pregnancy.
Gonorrhea Burning or frequent urination, a yellowish vaginal discharge, redness and swelling of the genitals, and a burning or itching of the vaginal area.
HPV Appearance of a genital lesion. Genital warts usually appear as small, fleshy, raised bumps on sexually-exposed area and are sometimes associated with itching, burning, or tenderness in the area.
Genital herpes Initial itching or tingling sensation followed by redness of the skin. Then a blister forms, followed by ulcers when the blisters break.
STD Symptoms for Boys
Chlamydia Many do not display any symptoms until severals weeks of infection. Pain when urinating, penile discharge, swelling of testicles.
Gonorrhea Pain when urinating, a green, white, or yellow discharge from the penis, swollen testicles.
Hepatitis B Loss of appetite, lethargic feelings, low-grade fever, muscle and joint pain and aches, nausea, vomiting and jaundice (yellow hue to the skin and dark urine).
Herpes Tingling, itching, or burning of the skin in the area around the blisters on the penis, testicles, on and around the anus, buttocks, or thighs or blisters on the lips, tongue, gums, and other parts of the body.

Q:

Hi I’m Xavier. Tonight is my prom night and my date, Daniel, and I might have sex tonight. What do I need to keep in mind before having sex?

A:

Before having sex, ask your partner if they currently have an STD. You should know if you have an STD yourself to that you can also protect your partner’s health. If either person is unsure, the best way to be safe is to always wear a condom. You need to be especially careful in your case because in sexual activities between males and males (anal sex), there are higher chances of transmission of sexual diseases because the digestive tracts will absorb both the nutrients and the HIV virus from outside. Always protect yourself even if you have sex with your long-term partner.

Q:

Tonight is an important night. It’s our 2 months anniversary and we are going to have sex … I’m worried, how should I prepare myself so that I won’t get pregnant?

A:

As a woman there are multiples steps you can take in order to avoid an unwanted pregnancy that do not rely on the man using birth control or withdrawing. For example, you can get a birth control implant, a by small-sized rod that gets inserted in the arm or the uterus by a health care provider. There is also a birth control ring that gets placed in your virgina once a month for three weeks to prevent pregnancy.

Q:

Oh it seems painful. Is there any other way, Doctor?

A:

You can also take birth control in the form of either pills or shots. The shot only lasts for three months. There are two types of pills right now:

Birth control pills: which you need to take everyday around the same time. These are low dosages of hormones that regulate your periods and are the most common birth control option prescribed by health providers.

Morning pills: to be used only the case of an emergency if you had unprotected sex and believe you could be pregnant. Must be taken up to 5 days after you had sex.

Q:

Is there a chance birth control can fail?

A:

Nothing is completely 100% success. Birth control implants have the best success rate in women but if you never want to become pregnant, you can choose to do tubal sterilization. However, it is permanent.

Q:

Hi Doctor, I’m Ellen. I think I got an STD. Can you tell me what the usual symptoms of STDs are?

A:

Each STD has different symptoms. Some common STDs for women are chlamydia, gonorrhea, HPV, and genital herpes. The list I provided on top of this page briefly details some symptoms for each STD, but I think you should to go to the hospital to get tested if you are still unsure.

Q:

Doctor, this is me, Allison and my friend Bob. We just got the results back- we both have an STD!! Where should I get my treatment?

A:

I can give you the list of places to go to. Some diseases such as chlamydia, syphilis, and gonorrhea can be cured by antibiotic treatment. However, HIV or chronic hepatitis B infection don’t have cures yet. But don’t lose hope but you can take medications to manage these chronic conditions. If you have an STD, make sure you tell your previous partners so that they can go get checked and treated as well.

Q:

I just found out we have an STD (Nooo!!!). What should I do so that my family/friends don’t get STD from me?

A:

STDs involve the transmission of a disease from one person to another during sexual activity. So all you need to do is to avoid sexual contact until to STD has been cured. Sexual contact is more than just sexual intercourse (vaginal and anal). It even includes kissing, oral-genital contact, and the use of sexual "toys," such as vibrators.

Q:

Hi doctor, I’m Taylor. I just met a really cool guy yesterday at a party, and our relationship is progressing. One problem, I have STD, but I don’t want him to get it from me. How can we do it safely?

A:

Hi Taylor, condoms are the only birth control that reduces your risk of both pregnancy and STDs. But, in order to work, condoms must be used correctly and must be used every time you have sex. It’s important to know, however, that they cannot completely protect you and your partner from some STDs, like herpes, syphilis, or human papilloma virus (HPV), the virus that causes genital warts and cervical cancer. Also, condoms can break, slip, or leak, especially if they are not put on and taken off properly. The only sure way to prevent pregnancy and STDs is to NOT to have sex and make sure you get regularly tested and treated for your current STD.

Q:

Hi Doctor. Last night, I went to the bar, got drunk, and this morning, I woke up seeing a stranger in my bed. Apparently we had sex last night, without using any protection devices. I don’t know if they have an STD or not. What should I do now?

A:

If you do not want to get pregnant, you can take a morning after pill. Since you don’t know about your partner’s health condition, you should go tested for STDs as soon as possible. If you have sex without using any protection device and your sexual partner has an STD, it is likely that the disease is transmitted.
